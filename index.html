My girlfriend Aaradhya and I have been together for eight months. We’ve FaceTimed once. We live 2,083 kilometers apart. And on Thursday, she has an exam that represents a significant Q1 deliverable for her academic career.

The standard narrative here is anxiety. But here’s what I’m realizing: When you can’t physically be with someone, you are forced to conduct a different kind of due diligence. You see what they’re actually made of.

And what I see is someone who has been crushing it this whole time, whether she realizes it or not.

The acquisition strategy: Cold outreach via Instagram

We met the modern way: Instagram DMs. Eight months ago, I initiated contact with zero expectations of a return on investment. She replied. That reply pivoted into a conversation, which scaled rapidly into daily voice notes.

Those voice notes now occupy roughly 60% of my phone’s total storage capacity. That is a hard data point that proves just how much we have to say to each other.

Now, I track her flights like a logistics manager. I know her coffee order. I’ve watched her explain cellular respiration with the same energy most people reserve for a viral Netflix series. I’ve heard her break down biology concepts, Harry Potter lore, and murder mysteries at hours when most people are offline.

Somewhere in that process, I fell completely in love with someone I’ve technically seen in person zero times.

Distance forces you to audit character

When you’re in the same city, you can rely on "presence bias"—hanging out effectively masks compatibility issues. Long distance doesn't give you that luxury. You have to look at the fundamentals.

Here is a specific use-case that defines Aaradhya:

I once mentioned, casually, that I needed nail clippers. It was a throwaway comment. She remembered. She bought them. She handled the shipping logistics to get them 2,083 kilometers to me.

That isn't just a gift; it's a signal of high-level competence and care. She pays attention to the small data. She manages family dynamics, friends, school, and a boyfriend who is objectively intense, and she does it while remaining genuinely kind. That is not a performance. That is her baseline operation.

Why I’m not focused on Thursday's results

She’s stressed about the exam. I can tell from the text frequency and tone. The exam is real, and the stakes are high.

But I am not going to offer empty corporate platitudes like "you’ve got this." Instead, I am looking at her track record.

I’ve watched her handle eight months of distance, academic pressure, and the complexity of our situation. She is already doing the hard thing. The exam on Thursday is just one day. It measures recall under pressure. It does not measure the soft skills that actually make a person valuable to have in your life.

Here is what exams fail to measure:

  • Character resilience.
  • Emotional intelligence.
  • The ability to maintain a relationship across time zones.
  • How you treat stakeholders (people) when you are stressed.
  • Whether you are worth knowing.

Aaradhya is worth knowing. That valuation is stable. It is not contingent on Thursday’s results.

The view from 2,083 kilometers away

I notice how she talks about biology like it’s fascinating—because to her, it is. That curiosity is a competitive advantage. I notice how she manages everything without complaining. She just executes.

I notice that she’s beautiful, obviously, but the real value is in how she thinks and how she shows up.

I’m not worried about the exam. I’m already proud of her for the operational resilience she shows every single day. Her value is inherent. It exists independent of external validation or test scores.

The post-exam outlook

Thursday will come and go. The exam will be over. Life will continue.

And I’ll still be here, 2,083 kilometers away, tracking her flights and listening to her voice notes, waiting for the day when the distance metric finally hits zero.

Until then, I’m just watching someone I love be brilliant, kind, and resilient. That is enough. She is enough.

The market conditions may change on Thursday, but my investment in her? That’s holding steady forever.
